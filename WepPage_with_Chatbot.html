    • Welcome & Getting Ready

      FAQ

      • Do I have to prepare anything? -- Yes, whenever we talk about case studies you have to read and study them upfront and answer the online quiz. In other sessions, there is no need for a prepration.
      • How many hours will this course be each week (my TUM online schedule shows quite a lot)? -- There will be 4 academic hours of lecture (mandatory) and 2 academic hours of (voluntary) exercise. To facilitate better feedback, we offer two groups of exercises (same content) and one group of the free Q&A. 
      • Do I need to participate in the exercise? -- We strongly recommend so, yes. 
      • How will I be assigned to one of the three exercise groups? -- You can join any one of them flexibly to fit your schedule.
      • Is this module for students in Heilbronn only? -- Yes. Participation on-site is required.


      • Our top five recommendations for successful course participation are:

      • Visit Moodle regularly to be up-to-date (I will also upload some slide decks after lectures to fill out the gaps). 
      • We offer plenty of learning opportunities - use those that best suit your learning style. There is the class (pretty helpful and interactive, hopefully fun), there is the exercise (directly applying the theory to questions that are highly relevant for the exam), there is a massive amount of detailed videos on Moodle (over 80 this year, ideal for taking a deeper dive and recapping), there are many references to literature (in case you prefer books and papers or want to dive even deeper into the material), and there are interactive forums on Moodle (please ask any time). We use teaching case studies to dive deeper into applying your knowledge and discussing the limitations of our concepts and tools. You do not have to use this all - finding your best approach is up to you. We recommend: trying it all and seeing for yourself.
      • No preparation is required to participate in the lectures (except during the case study weeks in which you have to prepare the case in advance). In the case study weeks, please read (in advance) the case descriptions carefully and prepare all case-related questions. This way, we can have an interactive case discussion.
      • Prepare the tasks provided for the exercise sessions by recapping the lecture contents (videos and slides).
      • Actively participate in the exercises to have the best learning experience possible and prepare for the exam.

    • 03: Managing Inventory under Uncertainty

      The Newsvendor Model is THE inventory model for modeling uncertain demand. Are there more models? Yes. But certainly, this one sets the gold-standard. A must learn. In the lecture session, you will learn about the newsvendor situation, where the model applies, learn more about the underlying mathematics and how to derive the optimal solution. And finally you will learn how to apply it.

      You will note that when modeling uncertain demand with random variables, we need a bit of probability theory. In the first half of the session this week, I assume you are well trained in probability theory and know how to work with random variables (e.g., you know what a uniform distribution is and how to calculate with a normally distributed random variable). 

      Based on my experience at TUM in the previous years and my experience at other schools, this may or may not be a valid assumption. Therefore, I created a quite comprehensive session entitled: "Modelling uncertain demand with random variables", which we will cover in the session as well.

    • 04: Economic Order Quantity Model, Production Systems and Batching

        Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) Model

      When it comes to inventory management, by now, you are familiar with the newsvendor model and with modeling uncertain demand using random variables.

      In this session, we are covering deterministic and constant demand. That is, you know what you are going to sell and there are no order fluctuations. Trivial? Not at all. In this model, we assume a multi-period context. That is, instead of ordering for one specific selling season as in the newsvendor model, you consider an infinite time horizon placing multiple orders: How frequently should you order and how much each time?

      We cover the so called EOQ model on the first day this week. Later, we talk about production systems and you will learn how the EOQ model can be adapted to identify optimal lot sizes for production. And, even more, we will then challenge the concept of batching when learning more about lean management.

    • 07: Supply Chain Coordination

      SC Coordination

      This session starts with the supply chain simulation called beer game. We will also discuss the results and implications. The Supply chain simulation beer game can be found here: https://scm-webinars.org/beergame

      The ensuing discussion builds on the effects observed in the beer game. It is on supply chain coordination. You will learn that the effect observed in the beer game is called "bullwhip effect". You will learn where it occurs in industry, about further causes and mitigation strategies. We will cover most theory in class. 


      Mandatory readings

    • 08: Facility Location Problems & Transportation and Shipping

      Facility and Location Problems are inherently strategic: where should you locate your plants and warehouses to efficiently serve the market? Yet, as we shall see in this unit, they also comprise operational decision elements. Assuming you pick a given location, how would this impact your daily decisions? Bridging both levels - strategic and operational decision making - is what we do in this section.

      You are probably familiar with the basic concepts of management science and so the first part of the session will be straight forward. However, we are soon covering models that are likely new to you and that require you to pay close attention. Keep in mind that these models (despite their specific names) are still generic. In real-world applications, multiple adjustments, relaxations and added constraints are required. Understanding the logic inherent to these models is an important start.

      Transportation and Shipping

      More on a tactical or operational level, you need to decide how to transport and ship your products. Introducing the broad class of transportation and shipping problems, we discuss three fundamental problems.

      The shortest path problem is a simple problem; one that we can solve optimally through a greedy heuristic. However, clearly not all network problems are that simple and, as an example for a hard problem, we discuss the travelling salesmen problem.

      • 13: Final wrap-up, Exam Preparation, Outlook

        In the last session, we had a brief recap of the entire course and I shared some facts about the exam such as what types of question there will be (i.e., multiple choice, calculation tasks, and text answers). I also mentioned that essentially all that we have covered is relevant. Some of you asked me for more exercises to practice for the exam and after giving it some thought I decided that this might be helpful. So I created this online "unit" with questions and answers. I believe that solving questions and getting feedback (even though only to a limited extent) is more helpful than just having questions without any form of feedback.

        Having said that, I was a bit torn apart. On the one hand, more training is great and I totally support that. On the other hand, my experience is that providing a mock-up exam or exam-like questions almost always leads to confusion: some students believe that it suffices to focus on these questions, others feel these questions as too difficult/confusing (at the end of the day, as a professor, you always spent much more time on the real exam questions than on mock-up exam questions). The truth is: the questions here are meant as training questions and exam questions will differ. But - of course - what would be the point in creating these questions for training if they were not related to the exam at all? 

        Here are a few important notes that I want you to read carefully to avoid confusion and prepare you best for the exam.

        1. In this part, there are exam-like questions. Not exam-questions. Still they are relevant.
        2. I tend to be more thorough in exam questions than in training questions. If you feel that 1 or 2 questions in this section are ambiguous, I am sorry for that. But rest assured, exam questions are clearer.
        3. I am only asking closed-answer, mostly multiple choice (MC), questions in this online part so that you get immediate feedback. In the exam there are MC questions, open-ended text answer questions (e.g. “explain…”) and calculation related questions.
        4. I came up with some questions; by no means do I want to imply that their amount is typical for the exam. By nature, they are distinct from exam questions since online I am only using MC questions and fill-in-the-gap kind of questions that can be automatically corrected. The exam is designed such that everyone who is well-prepared and knows the answers can finish well in time. But there is not much time to waste.
        5. I am not asking any case related questions here (i.e., in this online unit) as we already have some of them online. Just keep in mind that there will be some case related questions in the exam.
        6. Moodle asks me go give points for correct answers, hence I am giving points. Honestly, though, this is mostly random as they do not count (you will see that some fill-in-the-gaps questions here lead to an incredible amount of points whereas calculation tasks to only very few; that totally does not matter, in the exam this is different). Please keep in mind that in the exam you will get roughly 1 point per 1 minute that I think a task/question will take.
        7. Note: this online "test" is purely for training purposes. You will not get any extra points in the exam. Vice-versa, if you do not work with these online tests, you are not loosing any points for the exam.
        8. My recommendation for studying is, as always, (i) read the material, your notes, and the cases carefully. (ii) Repeat all exercises we did in class. (iii) Form a small study group with fellow students to discuss these exercises and the material. If you identify gaps, close them. (iv) Once you feel well-prepared, use these online questions to test your knowledge. (v) If you can answer them immediately, that is great. If not, try to close the gaps first, then answer the questions without you notes. (vi) carefully study your errors and identify gaps to learn more. (vii) Relax – you should be well prepared.
